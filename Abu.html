Mount Abu
Forget the hustle and bustle of city life for a sec! Mount Abu offers way more than just serene lakes. Imagine a sanctuary perched amidst a peak, untouched by the scorching desert heat. Yep, Mount Abu, at 1,200 meters above sea level, boasts a cool and refreshing climate, a welcome respite from the Thar Desert below!

Abu and it's paradise

Mount Abu's peaks pierce the clouds, offering breathtaking vistas that will leave you speechless. Imagine sprawling valleys painted emerald green by lush forests, all the way to the distant plains shimmering in the desert sun. Hike to the highest point, Guru Shikhar, and witness a panoramic tableau that stretches for miles. Each turn on a winding trail unveils a new scene, a breathtaking composition of nature's grandeur. Whether you seek a sunrise spectacle bathed in golden hues or a sunset symphony painted with fiery colors, Mount Abu's breathtaking views promise to captivate every traveler's heart.

Let's Go Back in Time

  • Ancient Abode: Mount Abu's story stretches back millennia, whispered to be a sacred refuge since time immemorial. Legends speak of sages like Vashishtha seeking solace in its peaceful embrace. The name itself, translating to "Abu's Mountain," likely originates from Arbuda, a serpent revered in ancient mythology.
  • Jain Pilgrimage Site: Over the centuries, Mount Abu flourished as a Jain pilgrimage center. From the 2nd to the 15th centuries AD, numerous Jain temples were constructed, celebrated for their intricate marble carvings and exquisite workmanship. Dilwara Temples, intricately adorned with mythical figures and floral patterns, stand as a testament to this rich Jain heritage.
  • Medieval Hill Fort: In the 14th century, Rajput rulers recognized Mount Abu's strategic significance. Rao Lumba of the Deora-Chauhan dynasty conquered the region and established the Achalgarh Fort, a formidable structure perched atop a granite cliff. The fort's ramparts and watchtowers bear witness to a bygone era of battles and conquests.
  • British Summer Escape: In the 19th century, the British discovered Mount Abu's cool climate as a welcome respite from the scorching Indian plains. They established Mount Abu as a hill station, building colonial bungalows, churches, and a polo ground. This era left a distinct mark on the landscape, with its charming colonial architecture blending seamlessly with the natural beauty.
  • Modern Transformation: After Indian independence, Mount Abu evolved into a popular tourist destination. Today, it's known for its serene lakes, breathtaking viewpoints, and rich cultural heritage. Whether you're a spiritual seeker, a nature enthusiast, or a history buff, Mount Abu offers a captivating glimpse into its ancient past nestled amidst breathtaking surroundings.
