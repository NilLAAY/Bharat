History


Indus Valley's Forgotten Metropolis

  • Early Human Presence: Humans have inhabited South Asia for at least two million years.

  • Shifting Lifestyles: By 30,000 years ago, stone age hunter-gatherers were established in the region.

  • Rise of Agriculture: Between 8000 and 6500 BCE, people transitioned from relying on wild resources to domesticating plants and animals.

  • Urbanization Emerges:Highly organized settlements flourished in the northern regions (present-day Pakistan and North India) between 5000 and 2000 BCE.

Indus Valley Civilization and the Rise of Indo-Aryan Culture

  • Emergence of Indus Valley Civilization: Around 2600 B.C.E., regional cultures in the Indus Valley region were unified into a culturally integrated network, spanning a vast area of 650,000 square kilometers.

  • Shared Cultural Characteristics: The people of the Indus Valley shared several cultural traits, such as planned urban developments.

  • Decline of the Indus Valley Civilization: The Indus Valley cultural system began to decline in the early centuries of the second millennium B.C.E.

  • Rise of Indo-Aryan Culture: Around 1500 B.C.E., Indo-Aryan culture started to dominate the region. This culture is associated with Sanskrit, a language related to Greek, Latin, and Avestan.

The Early and Classical Periods

  • Early Human Presence: Humans have inhabited South Asia for at least two million years.

  • Shifting Lifestyles: By 30,000 years ago, stone age hunter-gatherers were established in the region.

  • Rise of Agriculture: Between 8000 and 6500 BCE, people transitioned from relying on wild resources to domesticating plants and animals.

  • Urbanization Emerges:Highly organized settlements flourished in the northern regions (present-day Pakistan and North India) between 5000 and 2000 BCE.

The "Medieval" Period

  • Regional Centers and Lack of Central Authority: Strong regional centers emerged, with no overarching political authority in the subcontinent.

  • Muslim Polity in Sind: Sind in present-day Pakistan became integrated into a Muslim polity, while Turkic and Central Asian invasions commenced.

  • Turkish Invasions and Regional Kingdoms: Turkish invasions established centralized powers based in Delhi, while independent regional kingdoms continued to flourish.

  • Rajput and Regional Kingdoms: Rajput rulers in Rajasthan and Punjab established powerful kingdoms alongside other regional powers.


The Mughals

  • Foundation of the Mughal Empire: Babur, a Turkish/Central Asian chieftain, established the Mughal empire in 1526, with roots tracing back to Chingiz Khan and Timur.

  • Expansion under Akbar: Akbar expanded Mughal rule across the north, incorporating Rajput rulers through diplomatic means.

  • Influence in the Punjab Hills: Mughal influence extended to the Rajput hill-states of the Punjab hills (now Himachal Pradesh) during Jahangir's reign.

  • Consolidation of Mughal Power: Mughal rulers consolidated power through administrative reforms and diplomatic alliances.


British Rule

  • Establishment of British Control: British rule in South Asia began in the 18th century, gradually expanding as Mughal control declined.

  • Battle of Plassey: After the Battle of Plassey in 1757, the British gained control of Bengal, marking a significant step in their colonial expansion.

  • First War of Indian Independence: The First War of Indian Independence in 1857 marked a turning point as British control was solidified.

  • Quasi-Independent Rulers: Despite British dominance, some regions remained under quasi-independent rulers, albeit under British influence.


The Modern Nation-States

  • Partition of India and Pakistan: In 1947, India and Pakistan (East and West) were formed out of the British empire in India, accompanied by violence and displacement.

  • Formation of Bangladesh: In 1971, East Pakistan became independent as Bangladesh following a liberation war.

  • Cultural and Historical Ties: Despite political divisions, the nations of South Asia share cultural and historical ties, contributing to a complex regional identity.

  • Diaspora Community: South Asians in various parts of the world form a dynamic Diaspora community, maintaining connections with their homeland.
